The SummerLee Foundation

The project was a result of researchers: Darah Vann, Joanna Lovejoy, Mark Musquiz, Michael Smith, Nydia Hernandez, and Patricio De Jesus. The project was sponsored by the Summerlee Foundation, and it was established in 1988 by Annie Lee Roberts. The SummerLee Foundation is highlighting the crucial history that has had a degree of influence on shaping the history of Texas. Most individuals may have trouble understanding how history played a role in shaping modern society, but the Summerlee foundation definitely assist in clarifying responsible influences. Thanks to their research, we have an opportunity to see how owners viewed slaves and how the times were different compared to the present.

Description of database

The information organized on the website features a simple layout. Top most portion of the website presents the title of the project, “Texas Runaway Slave Project” below which shows “East Texas Research Center” following the subtitle, four database collections falling under The Texas Runaway project, and East Texas Digital Archives can be found. Under these links, a description of what the database consists of is summarized. Advertisement, articles, and notices are presented in a horizontal fashion, under the subtitle “CONTENT”. Next to which a filter, metadata, and interactive collection of maps are presented under the subtitle “BROWSE”. The right-most area of the website presents the subtitle “ABOUT” under which contacts (providing contact of project manager), Content manager (under named Dillon Wackerman), Project Information (links named “Issues Reviewed” and “Methodology”), Research Tools and Links (various links that aided in the ultimate research of the Texas Runaway Slave Project). 
 Users who visit the site can read the already presented information, but they also have the opportunity to seek out more. The information that the user may require can be accessed by links provided throughout the webpage. Although the database provides users this information, it also adds to society's historical knowledge. The historical knowledge such as: the information it presents is in a clarified and organized manner from 1800’s, that otherwise could be difficult to find. It’s a great way to give information back to society that may be difficult to find elsewhere.

Where to run to... and why?

It is around the time the emancipation was established, the 13th amendment followed being officially passed in 1865 which meant the end of slavery. According to the database, between the 1830’s and 1869 most slaves were assumed to have fled to Mexico. This is indicated by a comparison of locations where slaves had been captured and where the slave owners inhabited. Through the advertisements that were provided; sheriffs, slave owners, and civilians would be able to recognize a slave, at which point might led to the slaves future capture. Slavery has been abolished in Mexico in 1829, and by then, Mexican President, Vicente Guerrero. Reynaldo Leanos, an independent journalist who has worked on KUT's Texas Standard (a media newcast that report on anything relating to Texas) provides a reason for the slaves journeying to mexico: “Slaves in the US famously took the underground railroad north into free states and Canada, but a similar path existed to the south into Mexico. Slavery was abolished in Mexico in 1829 by Mexican President Vicente Guerrero, who was of mixed descent, including African heritage.” Bacha-Garza, a researcher, educated with a MA in history, includes her interpretation in regards to slaves and Mexico: “much of her research comes from oral histories of descendants of families who originally settled on the border during this time period, and through historical documents. She believes, because Jackson Ranch was so close to the Rio Grande, they would help smuggle slaves into Mexico. The Jacksons were religious people trying to do the right thing and did not believe in the Confederate cause.”
